# Voice-Over Script: The Basic Economic Problem (Cambridge Economics 9708)

**[Visual: Title card — “9708 Topic 1: The Basic Economic Problem”]**

**Narrator:**  
Welcome. This lesson is about the foundation of economics in your Cambridge 9708 course: **the basic economic problem**. By the end, you should be comfortable defining **scarcity**, listing the **factors of production**, explaining **opportunity cost**, and drawing a **production possibility curve** with clear exam-style labels.

---

**[Visual: Simple split screen — “Unlimited wants” vs “Finite resources”]**

**Narrator:**  
People and societies have wants that stretch far beyond what is available at any moment. **Resources are finite**, but **wants are effectively unlimited**. That gap is **scarcity**. Because of scarcity, we cannot produce everything; we must **choose**. Those choices show up as three classic questions: **what** to produce, **how** to produce it, and **for whom** to produce.

---

**[Visual: Icons for hospital, school, road — caption: “Government chooses between competing uses of tax revenue”]**

**Narrator:**  
Most goods you meet in exams are **economic goods**: getting more of them means **giving something else up**, because resources could have been used differently. A **free good** is illustrated as something available in such abundance that **extra use** does not force that sacrifice—examiners often use **clean air in an unpolluted area** as an example. Always read the **case study**: pollution or congestion can turn a “free” resource into a **scarce** one.

---

**[Visual: Four labelled cards — Land / Labour / Capital / Enterprise, with Rent / Wages / Interest / Profit]**

**Narrator:**  
Economists group resources into four **factors of production**. **Land** covers natural resources; its reward is typically **rent**. **Labour** is human effort, paid **wages**. **Capital** means man-made tools, machines, buildings used in production—not cash sitting in a wallet. The return to capital is often linked to **interest** in introductory work. **Enterprise** is risk-taking and organisation; the reward is **profit**. Remember **human capital**: education and training make labour more productive and can move an economy’s **potential** output over time.

---

**[Visual: Simple choice animation — “Option A selected; Option B fades” with label “Next best forgone = opportunity cost”]**

**Narrator:**  
Whenever you choose, you give up the **next best alternative**. That value is **opportunity cost**. In essays, spell out the **real** alternative—foregone healthcare, leisure, or investment—not only the money figure. On a **PPC**, moving to produce more of one good means producing less of the other along the frontier; that **trade-off** **is** opportunity cost in the model.

---

**[Visual: PPC diagram — bowed-out curve, points inside (inefficient), on (efficient), outside (unattainable)]**

**Narrator:**  
The **production possibility curve** shows the **maximum** combinations of two types of output possible when resources and technology are fixed and **fully efficiently** employed. Points **on** the curve are **productively efficient**. **Inside** the curve means **waste** or **unemployment**—you could have more of both goods. **Outside** is **impossible** until the economy’s capacity grows.

---

**[Visual: Same PPC; arrow sliding along curve vs whole curve shifting outward]**

**Narrator:**  
A **movement along** the curve is a **reallocation** between the two goods. An **outward shift** means **growth in potential output**—for example more capital, better technology, or a better-educated workforce. Do not confuse **recovering** unused capacity toward the frontier with **shifting** the frontier itself; exams love that distinction.

---

**[Visual: Concave PPC with steeper slope as you add more of Good X — caption: “Increasing opportunity cost”]**

**Narrator:**  
The usual **bowed-out** shape reflects **increasing opportunity cost**: resources are not equally good at every task, so each extra unit of one good costs **more and more** of the other. A **straight-line** PPC is a simplified case of **constant** opportunity cost.
