# Complete Lecture: The Basic Economic Problem (Cambridge Economics 9708)

This lecture maps to **Topic 1: The basic economic problem** in the Cambridge International AS & A Level Economics (9708) specification. You should be able to define key terms, draw and interpret production possibility curves, and use **opportunity cost** in short answers and data-response questions.

---

## 1. What is “the economic problem”?

Economics begins with a tension:

- **Human wants** are effectively **unlimited** (we can always imagine more consumption, better quality, or new goods and services).
- **Resources** available to satisfy those wants are **finite** (limited in supply at any moment in time).

Because we cannot have everything we want, every society faces **scarcity**. **Scarcity** means that choices must be made: some wants will go unsatisfied, or satisfied only partially.

The **basic economic problem** is therefore: **how to allocate limited resources among competing uses** so that (as far as possible) wants are met.

Three fundamental questions follow from scarcity (you will see these in essays and structured questions):

1. **What** to produce? (Which goods and services, and in what quantities?)
2. **How** to produce? (Which methods, technology, and combination of resources?)
3. **For whom** to produce? (How is output distributed across people and groups?)

Different economic systems answer these questions through different institutions (markets, government, tradition), but **scarcity and choice** underlie all of them.

## 2. Economic goods and free goods

- An **economic good** (or **scarce good**) has a **positive opportunity cost**: obtaining more of it requires giving up something else because resources are limited. Most goods and services in exams are economic goods.
- A **free good** appears **unlimited at zero price** in the context of the question: extra consumption does not require sacrificing other goods. **Clean air** in an unpolluted setting is often used as an illustration; in reality many “free” resources become scarce once population and pollution are introduced—so always read the **scenario** the examiner gives.

**Exam tip:** If a resource becomes polluted or congested, it often **stops** being a free good because using it now implies a cost (health, time, cleanup).

---

## 3. Factors of production and their rewards

Economists classify **resources** (inputs) into four **factors of production**:

| Factor | Brief meaning | Reward (payment to the factor) |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------------|
| **Land** | Natural resources (including minerals, fertile soil, water, location) | **Rent** |
| **Labour** | Human effort (mental and physical) used in production | **Wages** |
| **Capital** | Man-made goods used to produce other goods and services (machinery, tools, buildings, infrastructure—not money in the bank) | **Interest** (in many syllabi; sometimes analysed as a return on capital more broadly) |
| **Enterprise** | Risk-taking and organisation; bringing the other factors together | **Profit** (normal and supernormal profit may be distinguished later in the course) |

**Human capital**—the skills and knowledge embodied in labour—is often stressed in data questions: education and training raise productivity and can shift production possibilities outward over time.

## 4. Opportunity cost

**Opportunity cost** is the **value of the next best alternative forgone** when a choice is made.

- It arises because of **scarcity**: choosing more of one thing means less of something else.
- It is expressed in terms of **real alternatives** (e.g. “foregone consumer goods,” “foregone leisure,” “foregone public services”), not only money.

**Worked idea (numerical intuition):**  
If a government spends an extra $100m on hospitals, the opportunity cost might be $100m less on education or infrastructure—whichever **next best** use the examiner’s context implies.

**PPC link:** When you move along a production possibility curve from one efficient combination to another, the opportunity cost of extra units of good X is **how much of good Y you give up** (often rising as you specialise more in X—see below).

## 5. Production possibility curves (PPCs)

A **production possibility curve** (also called a **production possibility frontier**, PPF) shows the **maximum** feasible combinations of two goods (or categories of output) an economy can produce in a period, **given**:

- the **quantity and quality** of factors of production, and  
- the **state of technology**,

and assuming all resources are **fully and efficiently** employed.

### 5.1 Shape and opportunity cost

- A **concave (bowed-out) PPC** from the origin reflects **increasing opportunity cost**: as you produce more of one good, you must give up **increasing** amounts of the other (resources are not equally suited to all uses).
- A **straight-line PPC** shows **constant opportunity cost** (a simplifying assumption sometimes used in multiple-choice items).

### 5.2 Points on the diagram

- **On** the frontier: **productively efficient**—no way to produce more of one good without producing less of another.
- **Inside** the frontier: **inefficient** use of resources (unemployment, underuse of capacity, poor organisation). Output could be increased without sacrifice.
- **Outside** the frontier: **unattainable** with current resources and technology.

### 5.3 Movements vs shifts

- **Movement along** the PPC: **reallocation** of existing resources between the two goods (a change in the economy’s **composition** of output).
- **Outward shift** of the PPC: **economic growth** in the sense of expanded potential—causes include more/better factors (e.g. investment, discovery of resources, improved education), technological progress, or improved efficiency that raises **potential** output.
- **Inward shift**: destruction of capital, emigration of skilled labour, war, or severe environmental damage reducing productive capacity.

**Important distinction:** A short-run **increase in actual output** up toward the frontier is **not** the same as shifting the frontier outward. Examiners often test whether you can separate **actual** vs **potential** output.

---

## 6. Specialisation and exchange (link to later topics)

Although **specialisation** and **trade** are developed further under allocation of resources and international trade, they connect logically here: when agents specialise according to **comparative advantage**, they can loosen the consumption limits implied by their own PPC—through **trade**, consumption possibilities can exceed isolated production possibilities. Keep this in mind for integrated answers that reference **why** economies trade.

---

## 7. Typical examination tasks

You should practise:

1. **Definitions:** scarcity, opportunity cost, factors of production, economic good, free good, productive efficiency.
2. **PPC diagrams:** correct axes labels, showing efficiency, inefficiency, and unattainable points; movements along vs shifts.
3. **Written explanation:** why PPCs are often bowed out; real-world causes of shifts; opportunity cost in government and consumer decisions.
4. **Data response:** interpret a table or short text using **explicit** opportunity-cost reasoning.

---

## Summary checklist

- Scarcity forces **what, how, for whom** decisions.
- Factors of production: **land, labour, capital, enterprise**—know typical **rewards**.
- **Opportunity cost** is the **next best alternative forgone** and underpins the PPC.
- **PPC**: boundary of **potential** output; concavity reflects **increasing opportunity cost**; distinguish **move along** vs **shift**.

**Next steps:** Apply these ideas to past-paper questions that combine the PPC with **market failure** or **government intervention** once you have studied those units—examiners reward **chains of reasoning** that start from scarcity and choice.
