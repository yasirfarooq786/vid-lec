# Voice-Over Script: Cell Structure (Cambridge International AS & A Level Biology 9700)

**[Visual: Title — “9700 Topic 1: Cell structure” — split: light microscope / plant and animal cells]**

**Narrator:**  
Topic **1** builds everything else in **9700**. Part **1.1** is **microscopes and measurement**. Part **1.2** is **what cells contain** and **how** those parts **work**. In exams you must separate **magnification** from **resolution**, carry out **graticule** reasoning, and **label** organelles with **accurate** functions.

---

**[Visual: Labelled compound microscope — arrows to eyepiece, objectives, stage, condenser, iris]**

**Narrator:**  
The **compound light microscope** combines an **eyepiece** and an **objective**. **Total magnification** is **eyepiece times objective**—for example ten times eyepiece with forty times objective gives **four hundred times**. The **condenser** and **iris diaphragm** adjust **light** and **contrast**. **Magnification** only enlarges the image; **fine detail** depends on **resolution**—how close two points can be and still look **separate**. Light microscopes hit a **resolution** limit set largely by the **wavelength** of light, so **extreme zoom** without enough resolution is just **empty magnification**.

---

**[Visual: TEM internal chloroplast vs SEM leaf surface — side labels]**

**Narrator:**  
**Electron microscopes** use **electron beams** and achieve **much higher resolution** than light. **Transmission EM** passes electrons through **ultra-thin** sections—powerful for **internal** organelle detail, often looking **two-dimensional**. **Scanning EM** scans surfaces—great for **three-dimensional** texture. Discuss **limitations**: specimens are usually **dead**, **processed**, sometimes **coated**; **artefacts** happen; equipment is **specialist** and **expensive**.

---

**[Visual: Eyepiece scale superimposed on stage micrometer — “align and count divisions”]**

**Narrator:**  
The **eyepiece graticule** is a **scale in the eyepiece**—its divisions are **arbitrary** until you **calibrate**. Use a **stage micrometer** slide with a **known** real scale. At a **fixed objective**, line up the graticule with the micrometer, count how many graticule divisions equal a **known micrometre distance**, then divide to get **micrometres per graticule division**. **Repeat calibration** whenever you change **objective**, because the **same** graticule tick now spans a **different** real width. To measure a cell, count **graticule divisions** across it and **multiply** by your **calibration**. Always state the **objective** you used.

---

**[Visual: Prokaryote simple diagram vs eukaryote — nucleus highlighted]**

**Narrator:**  
**1.2 Cells.** **Prokaryotes** lack a **true nucleus**—DNA is typically a **circular** chromosome in a **nucleoid**, with **70S ribosomes** and **no membrane-bound organelles** like mitochondria. A **bacterial cell wall** is often **peptidoglycan**. **Eukaryotes** package DNA in a **nucleus** with a **double** envelope and **pores**, and they contain **membrane-bound** compartments. **Plant** cells usually show a **cellulose wall**, **chloroplasts**, a **large vacuole** with **tonoplast**, and **plasmodesmata** between neighbours. **Animal** cells lack those plant features and commonly show **centrioles** in the **centrosome** region.

---

**[Visual: Animated tour — nucleus → RER → Golgi → vesicle]**

**Narrator:**  
The **nucleolus** inside the nucleus is central to **ribosomal subunit** assembly. **RER** is **rough** because of **ribosomes**—it makes and processes many **proteins** destined for **export**, **membranes**, or **lysosomes**. **SER** lacks those surface ribosomes—think **lipids**, **steroids**, and **detox** routes in **liver** contexts. The **Golgi** **modifies**, **sorts**, and **packages** into **vesicles**, including **lysosomal** precursors and **secretory** granules.

---

**[Visual: Mitochondrion cross-section — cristae; chloroplast — grana stacks]**

**Narrator:**  
**Mitochondria** have a **double membrane** and **cristae** for **aerobic respiration** and lots of **ATP** production; the **matrix** holds key **Krebs-cycle** chemistry. They carry **small circular DNA** and **70S ribosomes**—useful evidence when you discuss **evolution** of eukaryotes. **Chloroplasts** also have **double membranes**, **thylakoid grana** for **light-dependent** reactions, and **stroma** for the **Calvin cycle**; they too have **circular DNA** and **70S ribosomes**. Do **not** swap their **roles**.

---

**[Visual: Lysosome fusing with vesicle; centriole 9-triplet cylinder; cilium 9+2 cross-section]**

**Narrator:**  
**Lysosomes** contain **acid** **hydrolases** for **breakdown** after **fusion** with endocytosed material or during **autophagy**. **Centrioles** organise **microtubules**, important for **spindle** formation in many **animal** cells. **Cilia** often beat with a **nine-plus-two** microtubule layout to **move fluids**; **microvilli** are **membrane** folds that **boost absorption** surface area without the same motile machinery emphasis.

---

**[Visual: Plant cell wall with plasmodesmata channel — turgor arrow pressing membrane to wall]**

**Narrator:**  
The plant **cell wall** is **strong** and **permeable**, built from **cellulose** **microfibrils**. The **vacuole** stores **sap** and helps **turgor**; the **tonoplast** wraps it. **Plasmodesmata** link **cytoplasm** between cells for **transport** and **signalling**.
