## 1.1 The microscope in cell studies

### Light microscopy

The **compound light microscope** uses **visible light** and **glass lenses**. Typical parts you should be able to relate to use and image quality:

- **Eyepiece lens** and **objective lenses** (often ×4, ×10, ×40; ×100 oil immersion in some centres).
- **Stage** and **clips**, **condenser** and **iris diaphragm** (controls **light intensity** and **contrast**).
- **Coarse** and **fine** focusing controls.

**Magnification** (for the image you view) is calculated as:

**Total magnification = magnification of eyepiece × magnification of objective**

(e.g. ×10 eyepiece with ×40 objective → **×400**).

**Magnification** only makes the image **bigger**; it does **not** by itself add **detail** if the instrument cannot **resolve** fine structure.

### Magnification versus resolution

- **Resolution** is the ability to **distinguish two points** as separate. It is limited by the **wavelength** of the radiation used and the **quality** of the optics.
- The **light microscope** has a **limited resolution** (order of **~200 nm** in typical teaching contexts—sufficient for **cells** and **many organelles**, but not for **ribosome** detail).
- **Increasing magnification** beyond the point where resolution is limiting gives **empty magnification** (bigger but **blurrier**).

**Exam tip:** Define **both** terms; state that **resolution** limits **fine detail**, not just “zoom.”

### Electron microscopy (comparative awareness)

Electron microscopes use **beams of electrons** and **electromagnetic lenses**. They offer **much higher resolution** than light microscopes.

| Type | Typical use in teaching | Image character |
|------|-------------------------|-----------------|
| **TEM** (transmission EM) | Very **thin** sections; **internal** ultrastructure | Often **2D** appearance; high detail of organelles |
| **SEM** (scanning EM) | **Surface** architecture | **3D**-like topographic images |

**Limitations (for discussion):** specimens often **fixed**, **dehydrated**, **sectioned** (TEM) or **coated**; **vacuum** required; **artefacts** possible; **not** used for **living** cells in standard school contexts; **expensive** and **specialist** operation.

### Calibrating an eyepiece graticule with a stage micrometer

The **eyepiece graticule** is a **scale** in the eyepiece (often **100 arbitrary divisions**). These divisions do **not** correspond to a fixed real distance until **calibrated** at **each** objective magnification.

The **stage micrometer** is a slide with a **known scale** (commonly divisions of **0.1 mm (100 µm)** or finer etched spacings—**always read the scale printed on that slide**).

**Calibration procedure (logic):**

1. Place the **stage micrometer** on the stage and focus with the **objective** you will use (e.g. ×40).
2. Superimpose the **eyepiece graticule** scale on the **known** micrometer scale.
3. Count how many **graticule divisions** match a **known real distance** on the micrometer (convert mm → **µm** if needed: **1 mm = 1000 µm**).
4. Calculate **real distance per graticule division**:

**µm per graticule division = (known distance in µm) ÷ (number of graticule divisions aligned with that distance)**

5. Remove the stage micrometer, view your specimen at the **same** objective, measure the structure in **graticule divisions**, then:

**size of specimen (µm) = graticule divisions × calibrated µm per division**

**Worked example:** If **40** graticule divisions span **100 µm** on the stage micrometer, one division = **100 ÷ 40 = 2.5 µm**. A cell spanning **27** divisions → **27 × 2.5 = 67.5 µm**.

**Why re-calibrate?** Changing **objective** (or zoom on digital systems where applicable) changes how much of the specimen fits in one eyepiece division—**calibration is magnification-specific**.

**Practical reliability:** Use **fine focus** for a sharp image; **repeat** measurements; **avoid** parallax when aligning scales; record **which objective** was used.

## 1.2 Cells as the basic units of living organisms

### The cell as a unit of life

- The **cell** is the **smallest** unit that shows the **characteristics of life** in full (metabolism, response, growth/reproduction, homeostasis—depending on context).
- New cells usually arise from **division of existing cells** (reinforced when you study mitosis).
- **Viruses** are **not** cells (no metabolism independent of a host; structure is **nucleic acid + capsid**—covered in infectious diseases topic).

### Prokaryotic versus eukaryotic cells

| Feature | **Prokaryotic** (e.g. typical bacterium) | **Eukaryotic** (animal, plant, fungus, protist) |
|---------|------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| **Nucleus** | **No true nucleus**; **naked** circular DNA in **nucleoid** region | **True nucleus** with **nuclear envelope** |
| **Membrane-bound organelles** | **Absent** (no mitochondria, ER, Golgi, etc.) | **Present** |
| **Ribosomes** | **70S** (smaller) | **80S** in **cytoplasm** (see below for organelle exceptions) |
| **Cell wall** | Usually **peptidoglycan** (bacteria) | **Plants**: **cellulose**; **fungi**: **chitin**; animals: **no wall** |
| **Size** | Generally **smaller** (order of **1–10 µm** typical) | Generally **larger** (often **10–100 µm** for many study cells) |
| **Extra structures** | **Plasmid** DNA, **flagellum** (some), **capsule** (some), **pili** (some) | **Chloroplasts** (plants/algae), **lysosomes** (common in animals), etc. |

**Exam tip:** Use **“no membrane-bound organelles”** rather than vague “simple cell.”

### Animal versus plant cells (eukaryotic)

**Typical plant cell features** (often absent or different in animal cells):

- **Cellulose cell wall** for **strength**; **fully permeable** to water and solutes (unlike the **selectively permeable** membrane inside).
- **Chloroplasts** (photosynthesis in appropriate species/cells).
- **Large permanent vacuole** with **cell sap**; **tonoplast** (membrane around the vacuole) controls transport into/out of the vacuole; contributes to **turgor**.
- **Plasmodesmata**—**cytoplasmic connections** through cell walls linking **adjacent** plant cells.

**Animal cell features** often emphasised:

- **Centrioles** (paired, role in **formation of spindle** microtubules in many animal cells—plants lack centrioles in many specifications).
- **Lysosomes** prominent in teaching (though lysosome-like structures exist in plants with different terminology in some contexts).

## Organelles and structures (structure ↔ function)

Use this section as a **revision map**; draw each with **correct relative positions** and **labels**.

### Nucleus, nuclear envelope, nucleolus

- **Nuclear envelope:** **double membrane** with **nuclear pores**—controls **movement** of RNA and proteins between **nucleus** and **cytoplasm**; **chromatin** (DNA + proteins) inside.
- **Nucleolus:** site of **rRNA** transcription/assembly and **ribosomal subunit** formation (subunits exported to cytoplasm).

### Cell surface membrane

- **Phospholipid bilayer** with **proteins**; **selective barrier** between cell and environment (detailed **fluid mosaic** model is Topic **4**).

### Rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER)

- **Membrane-bound flattened sacs (cisternae)** with **ribosomes** on the **outer** surface.
- **Protein synthesis** and **folding**, especially proteins for **secretion**, **lysosomes**, or **insertion into membranes**; **large surface area**.

### Smooth endoplasmic reticulum (SER)

- **No ribosomes** on surface.
- **Lipid** and **steroid** synthesis; **calcium ion** storage in muscle (when relevant); **detoxification** of some substances in **liver** cells (e.g. enzymes that modify drugs/toxins).

### Golgi body (Golgi apparatus / Golgi complex)

- **Stacked flattened sacs (cisternae)**; **cis** (receiving) and **trans** (shipping) faces often described.
- **Modifies**, **packages**, **sorts** proteins and lipids into **vesicles**; formation of **lysosomes**; **secretory vesicles** for **exocytosis**.

### Mitochondria

- **Double membrane**; inner folded into **cristae** (increases **surface area** for **ATP** production); **matrix** contains enzymes for **link reaction** and **Krebs cycle** (respiration topic).
- **Aerobic respiration** (oxidative phosphorylation on cristae membranes).
- **Small circular DNA** and **70S ribosomes**—evidence supporting **endosymbiotic** origin in teaching.

### Ribosomes

- **Protein synthesis** (translation of mRNA).
- **80S** in **eukaryotic cytoplasm** (larger subunit + smaller subunit).
- **70S** in **mitochondria** and **chloroplasts** (matches prokaryotic-type machinery).

### Lysosomes

- **Spherical sacs** of **hydrolytic enzymes** (optimum **acidic** pH inside).
- **Intracellular digestion** (endocytosed material), **autophagy**, **recycling**; **fuse** with vesicles. **Stability** of enzymes until fusion is functionally important.

### Centrioles and microtubules

- **Centriole:** **9 triplets** of **microtubules** in a cylinder; in **animal cells** often **two** at right angles near the nucleus (**centrosome** region)—role in organising **microtubules** of the **spindle** in mitosis.
- **Microtubules:** **cytoskeleton** component; **tracks** for **organelle movement**; form **spindle fibres**; **9+2** arrangement in **cilia and flagella** (below).

### Cilia and microvilli

- **Cilia:** many, **short**, **hair-like**; **undulipodium**-type **9+2 microtubule** arrangement; often **move fluid** or mucus (e.g. epithelia); may be **non-motile** (**primary cilia**) in some specialised roles (extension level varies by syllabus emphasis).
- **Microvilli:** **finger-like projections** of the **cell surface membrane**; **increase surface area** for **absorption** (e.g. ileum epithelium).

### Chloroplasts

- **Double membrane**; **grana** (stacks of **thylakoids**) where **light-dependent** reactions occur; **stroma** contains enzymes for **Calvin cycle** (photosynthesis topic).
- **Chlorophyll** and accessory pigments in thylakoid membranes.
- **Small circular DNA** and **70S ribosomes**—again linked to **endosymbiotic** theory in teaching.

### Cell wall (plant)

- **Cellulose** **microfibrils** in a matrix; **mechanical strength** against **turgor**; **porous**—water and solutes pass to the membrane.

### Plasmodesmata

- **Channels** through adjacent plant cell walls with **desmotubule** (ER connection) in detail; allow **movement** of some molecules and **signalling** between cells.

### Large permanent vacuole and tonoplast

- **Vacuole** stores **cell sap** (solutes, pigments, waste); **tonoplast** is the **surrounding membrane**; maintains **turgor** against the **cell wall** for **rigidity** of non-woody plant tissues.

### Cytoplasm

- **Cytosol** (fluid) plus **organelles** (excluding nucleus—**nucleoplasm** is separate). Many **enzyme-catalysed** reactions occur here.

## Integration and common misconceptions

- **Bigger magnification** does not mean you can see **ribosomes** with a light microscope—**resolution** is the limit.
- **Cell wall** is **not** the same as **membrane**—**permeability** and **composition** differ.
- **Chloroplasts** and **mitochondria** both have **double membranes** and **circular DNA**—do **not** confuse their **functions**.
- **SER** vs **RER**: absence vs presence of **membrane-bound ribosomes** is the quick structural distinction.
- **70S ribosomes** appear in **prokaryotes**, **mitochondria**, and **chloroplasts**; **80S** in **eukaryotic cytoplasm**.
