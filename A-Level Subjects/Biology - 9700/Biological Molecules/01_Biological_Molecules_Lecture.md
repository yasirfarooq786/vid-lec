## 1. The Chemistry of Carbon
Before we look at the molecules themselves, we must talk about the star of the show: **Carbon**. 

Carbon is the backbone of organic molecules. Why is it so special? It has the ability to form **four strong covalent bonds** with other atoms. This allows it to form long chains, branched structures, and stable rings. Without carbon, complex life wouldn't be possible!

### *Monomers vs. Polymers*
Imagine a polymer as a pearl necklace.
* **Monomers:** These are the individual pearls (small, simple, single units).
* **Polymers:** This is the entire necklace (a large, complex molecule made by joining monomers together).

> **Teacher's Tip:** When monomers join together to form a polymer, they usually release a water molecule. This is called a **condensation reaction**. When a polymer is broken down into monomers, a water molecule is added. This is a **hydrolysis reaction** (hydro = water, lysis = break).

## 2. Carbohydrates: The Energy Providers
Whenever you eat pasta, bread, or fruit, you are consuming carbohydrates. They are made of Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), and Oxygen (O). They follow the general formula $(CH_2O)_n$.

We divide carbohydrates into three main groups:

### A. Monosaccharides (Simple Sugars)
These are single sugar units. The most important one is **Glucose**.
* **Glucose ($C_6H_{12}O_6$):** The primary energy source for cellular respiration. 
* *Alpha ($\alpha$) vs. Beta ($\beta$) Glucose:* Glucose comes in two forms depending on the position of the hydroxyl (-OH) group on Carbon-1. (In $\alpha$-glucose, it points *down*; in $\beta$-glucose, it points *up*).

### B. Disaccharides (Double Sugars)
Formed when two monosaccharides join via a **condensation reaction**, forming a **glycosidic bond**.
* **Maltose:** $\alpha$-glucose + $\alpha$-glucose (Found in germinating seeds)
* **Sucrose:** $\alpha$-glucose + fructose (Transported in plant phloem)
* **Lactose:** $\alpha$-glucose + galactose (Found in mammalian milk)

### C. Polysaccharides (Complex Sugars)
Long chains of monosaccharides. They are excellent for storage because they are large and insoluble (they don't affect the water potential of cells).
* **Starch:** Made of $\alpha$-glucose. Used for energy storage in *plants*. Composed of amylose (unbranched spiral) and amylopectin (branched).
* **Glycogen:** Made of $\alpha$-glucose. Used for energy storage in *animals*. Very highly branched for rapid energy release.
* **Cellulose:** Made of $\beta$-glucose. Provides structural support in *plant cell walls*. The $\beta$-glucose molecules flip alternately, allowing straight chains to form strong microfibrils via hydrogen bonding.

## 3. Lipids: Fats and Oils
Unlike carbohydrates, lipids are **non-polar** and **hydrophobic** (water-hating). They don't form polymers in the traditional sense. 

### A. Triglycerides (Fats and Oils)
Think of butter or olive oil. A triglyceride is made of:
* **One Glycerol molecule**
* **Three Fatty Acid chains**

They joined together in a condensation reaction, forming three **ester bonds**. 
* **Role:** They yield more than twice as much energy per gram as carbohydrates! They also provide insulation and buoyancy for marine mammals.

### B. Phospholipids (The Cell Border Control)
Take a triglyceride, remove one fatty acid, and replace it with a phosphate group. You now have a phospholipid.
* **Phosphate Head:** Hydrophilic (water-loving)
* **Fatty Acid Tails:** Hydrophobic (water-hating)

*Why is this important?* When placed in water, phospholipids spontaneously form a bilayer—this is the foundational structure of **all cell membranes**!

## 4. Proteins: The Molecular Machines
If DNA provides the blueprint, proteins are the workers. They are made of Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, AND sometimes Sulfur.

### Amino Acids: The Building Blocks
Proteins are polymers made of monomers called **amino acids**. There are 20 naturally occurring amino acids.
Each amino acid consists of:
1. A central carbon atom
2. An amine group ($-\text{NH}_2$)
3. A carboxyl group ($-\text{COOH}$)
4. An R group (The side chain that makes each amino acid unique)

Two amino acids join via a condensation reaction, forming a **peptide bond**.

### The 4 Levels of Protein Structure
1. **Primary Structure:** The specific linear sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain. (Determined by DNA).
2. **Secondary Structure:** The chain begins to fold due to hydrogen bonding. It can form an **$\alpha$-helix** (like a telephone cord) or a **$\beta$-pleated sheet** (like a folded fan).
3. **Tertiary Structure:** The 3D folding of the entire molecule. Held together by hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, disulfide bridges, and hydrophobic interactions. *This specific 3D shape decides the protein's function (e.g., the active site of an enzyme!)*
4. **Quaternary Structure:** When a protein consists of more than one polypeptide chain. 
   * **Example:** Haemoglobin, which has four chains and carries oxygen in our blood.

### Globular vs Fibrous Proteins
* **Globular Proteins:** Roughly spherical, soluble in water, have metabolic roles (e.g., Enzymes, Haemoglobin).
* **Fibrous Proteins:** Long, structural strands, insoluble in water (e.g., Collagen, which provides tensile strength in tendons and skin).

## 5. Biochemical Tests (Practical Labs)
As a biologist, you must know how to identify these molecules in the lab!

1. **Reducing Sugars (e.g., Glucose):** Add Benedict’s reagent and heat in a water bath. *(Positive = Blue to Brick-Red precipitate)*
2. **Non-Reducing Sugars (e.g., Sucrose):** Boil with hydrochloric acid (to hydrolyse it), neutralize with an alkali, then do the Benedict's test.
3. **Starch:** Add Iodine solution. *(Positive = Yellow/Brown to Blue-Black)*
4. **Lipids (Emulsion Test):** Mix sample with ethanol to dissolve the lipid, pour into cold water. *(Positive = Milky white emulsion)*
5. **Proteins (Biuret Test):** Add Biuret reagent. *(Positive = Light blue to Lilac/Purple)*

## Summary & Recap
* **Carbohydrates** are our primary energy source and storage (glucose, starch, glycogen) and provide plant structure (cellulose).
* **Lipids** are used for long-term energy storage and are vital for cell membranes.
* **Proteins** perform enormous varieties of roles built via 4 structures (Primary to Quaternary).
* **Water** behaves as the universal solvent giving rise to condensation and hydrolysis reactions.
