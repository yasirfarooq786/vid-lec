# Voice-Over Script: Biological Molecules (Cambridge International AS & A Level Biology 9700)

**[Visual: Title — “9700 Topic 2: Biological molecules” — icons for sugar, oil drop, protein, water]**

**Narrator:**  
This is Topic **2: Biological molecules** for Cambridge **9700**. We follow the syllabus blocks **2.1** tests, **2.2** carbohydrates and lipids, **2.3** proteins, and **2.4** water. Your goal is to **describe procedures**, **interpret results**, and **explain structure and function** in exam-style chains of reasoning.

---

**[Visual: Lab bench — four labelled test tubes: Benedict, iodine, ethanol + water, biuret]**

**Narrator:**  
Section **2.1** is about **identifying** molecules. **Benedict’s reagent** detects **reducing sugars**. You add reagent, **heat** in a **boiling water bath**, and look for the colour move from **blue** toward **green, yellow, orange, brick-red precipitate**. **No change** means **no reducing sugar** under those conditions. For **semi-quantitative** work, you compare **final colour** or **time to first colour change** against **standard concentrations** you prepared with **identical volumes and heating time**—and you explain why that is only an **estimate**.

---

**[Visual: Iodine bottle dropping onto starch — blue-black flash]**

**Narrator:**  
**Iodine solution** tests for **starch**. A **blue-black** colour is **positive**; staying **yellow-brown** is **negative**. Remember starch is a **storage polysaccharide** in plants—link the test to **amylose** when examiners ask *why* the colour appears.

---

**[Visual: Ethanol pour into water — milky swirl; “no naked flames” safety sticker]**

**Narrator:**  
The **emulsion test** detects **lipid**. Shake the sample with **ethanol**, then dilute with **water**. Lipids that dissolved in ethanol form a **milky emulsion** in water. **Ethanol is flammable**—keep **flames away**.

---

**[Visual: Biuret — alkaline tube, then blue drops turning purple]**

**Narrator:**  
The **biuret test** detects **peptide bonds**. Make the sample **alkaline**, then add **copper two sulfate** or use **Biuret reagent**. **Purple or lilac** means **protein** is likely present; **blue** means negative. This is about **bonds in the chain**, not one specific amino acid.

---

**[Visual: Flowchart — Benedict negative → acid heat → fizz neutralise → Benedict positive]**

**Narrator:**  
**Non-reducing sugars** such as **sucrose** fail Benedict’s until you **hydrolyse** them. The exam sequence is: **Benedict’s first**—if negative, take fresh sample, add **dilute acid**, **heat gently**, **neutralise** carefully—for example **hydrogencarbonate** until **fizzing stops**—then **Benedict’s again**. A **new positive** implies a **non-reducing disaccharide** was split into **reducing monosaccharides**.

---

**[Visual: α-glucose vs β-glucose ring — highlight C1 OH orientation]**

**Narrator:**  
**2.2 Carbohydrates.** Monosaccharides are single units—know **glucose** and the idea of **α** versus **β** glucose because **starch and glycogen** use **α-glucose**, while **cellulose** uses **β-glucose**. **Disaccharides** form by **condensation** with a **glycosidic bond** and water lost; **hydrolysis** adds water back. **Maltose** is reducing; **sucrose** is **non-reducing**—that matches the tests.

---

**[Visual: Starch branching vs cellulose microfibrils — simple diagram]**

**Narrator:**  
**Starch** stores energy in plants—**amylose** and branched **amylopectin**. **Glycogen** is animal storage, **highly branched** for quick glucose release. **Cellulose** is **β-1,4** chains, **hydrogen-bonded** into **microfibrils**—strong **plant cell walls**. Explain **why** cellulose is not digested by human **amylase**: **bond geometry** and **enzyme specificity**.

---

**[Visual: Triglyceride — glycerol + three tails — then phospholipid bilayer sketch]**

**Narrator:**  
**Lipids:** **fatty acids** may be **saturated** or **unsaturated**—link **packing** to **melting point**. **Triglycerides** are **glycerol** plus **three ester-linked fatty acids**—**energy storage**, **insulation**, **protection**. **Phospholipids** have **two tails** and a **phosphate head**—**amphipathic**—the chemical logic of **membranes** you will unpack fully in Topic **4**.

---

**[Visual: Amino acid general formula — peptide bond animation — 4-level folding diagram]**

**Narrator:**  
**2.3 Proteins.** Amino acids share a **central carbon** with **amino**, **carboxyl**, **hydrogen**, and **R group**. **Peptide bonds** join them into **polypeptides**. Learn **primary** as **sequence**, **secondary** as local **alpha helix** and **beta sheet** with **hydrogen bonds**, **tertiary** as overall **3D folding** with several bond types including **disulphide** where relevant, and **quaternary** where **multiple chains** meet—as in **haemoglobin**. Contrast **fibrous** structural proteins like **collagen** with **globular** proteins like **enzymes**.

---

**[Visual: Water molecules — δ+ and δ− — H-bonds — ice floating]**

**Narrator:**  
**2.4 Water.** **Polarity** creates **hydrogen bonding**. That explains **solvent properties** for **ions and polar metabolites**, **high specific heat** and **latent heat of vapourisation** for **temperature stability** and **cooling by evaporation**, **cohesion and adhesion** for **transport in xylem**, and why **ice floats**—important for **aquatic ecosystems**.

Next in the syllabus is **enzymes**—you will use today’s ideas about **protein shape** and **molecular interaction** immediately.
