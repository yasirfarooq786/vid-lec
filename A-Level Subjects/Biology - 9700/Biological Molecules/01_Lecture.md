## Why this topic matters

All living processes depend on a small set of molecular types: **carbohydrates**, **lipids**, **proteins**, **water**, and (later in the course) **nucleic acids**. In exams you must **define** terms precisely, **draw and interpret** simple structures, **explain** roles in cells and organisms, and **describe practical procedures** including **controls**, **safety**, and **how results are used** (including semi-quantitative work).

## 2.1 Testing for biological molecules

Biochemical tests use **specific reagents**; a **positive** result is a **clear, repeatable colour or appearance change**. Practical questions often ask you to **standardise** conditions (same volumes, heating time, concentration of reagent) so comparisons are valid.

### Benedict’s test (reducing sugars)

**Reducing sugars** (e.g. glucose, maltose) can **reduce** copper(II) ions in Benedict’s reagent to copper(I) oxide under alkaline, heated conditions.

**Procedure (typical):**

1. Add **Benedict’s reagent** (blue) to the sample in a test tube (often **equal volumes** of sample and reagent for comparability).
2. **Heat** in a **boiling water bath** for a set time (commonly a few minutes).

**Results:**

- **Positive:** colour changes through **green → yellow → orange → brick-red precipitate** (the exact shade depends on **concentration**).
- **Negative:** solution stays **blue**.

**Semi-quantitative Benedict’s test**

To **estimate** reducing sugar concentration (not an exact value):

- Prepare **standard solutions** of known concentrations (e.g. a serial set).
- Run Benedict’s test on each under **identical** volume, reagent amount, and heating time.
- Either **compare final colour** to a **colour chart** from the standards, or measure **time to first visible colour change** and relate that to standards.

**Exam focus:** Explain why the method is **semi-quantitative** (subjective colour judgement, timing errors, heat transfer variation) and how to **improve reliability** (replicates, same apparatus, blind comparison to standards).

### Iodine test (starch)

**Starch** forms a **blue-black** complex with **iodine in potassium iodide solution** (often called **iodine solution** in school labs).

**Procedure:** Add drops of iodine solution to the sample (or mix on a spotting tile).

**Results:**

- **Positive:** **blue-black**.
- **Negative:** remains **yellow-brown** (the colour of the iodine reagent).

**Note:** Starch is a **polysaccharide** storage molecule in plants; glycogen gives a **reddish-brown** tone with iodine in some contexts—know what your practical protocol states.

### Emulsion test (lipids)

Lipids are **soluble in ethanol** but when ethanol is mixed with water, **lipid forms tiny droplets**—a **milky emulsion**.

**Procedure (typical):**

1. Add **ethanol** to the sample and **shake** (lipid dissolves if present).
2. Pour or dilute into **cold water** (or add water and shake).

**Results:**

- **Positive:** **cloudy / milky white** emulsion.
- **Negative:** remains relatively **clear**.

**Safety:** Ethanol is **flammable**—no naked flames.

### Biuret test (protein / peptide bonds)

The **biuret reaction** detects **peptide bonds**. In alkaline conditions, **Cu²⁺** forms a complex giving a **purple / violet** colour.

**Procedure (typical):**

1. Add **sodium hydroxide** to make the mixture **alkaline**.
2. Add a few drops of **copper(II) sulfate** solution and mix gently (or use **Biuret reagent** prepared for this purpose).

**Results:**

- **Positive:** **purple / lilac**.
- **Negative:** stays **blue**.

**Note:** Very short peptides may give weaker results; the test is **protein-focused** in most 9700 contexts.

### Non-reducing sugars (e.g. sucrose)

**Non-reducing disaccharides** do **not** give a positive Benedict’s test until they are **split** into **monosaccharides**.

**Principle:** **Acid hydrolysis** breaks the **glycosidic bond**; after **neutralisation**, **Benedict’s test** detects the **reducing monosaccharides** released.

**Logical sequence:**

1. Benedict’s test on original sample → if **positive**, reducing sugars already present.
2. If **negative**, take fresh sample, add **dilute acid**, **heat gently** to hydrolyse.
3. **Neutralise** carefully (e.g. **solid sodium hydrogencarbonate** until effervescence stops—**avoid dangerous splashing**; your centre may use pH monitoring).
4. Repeat **Benedict’s test** → **positive** after hydrolysis suggests a **non-reducing sugar** was present.

## 2.2 Carbohydrates and lipids

### Carbohydrates

**General formula** is often approximated as **C(H₂O)ₙ** (carbon “with water”); many carbohydrates are **polymers** built from **monosaccharide** monomers.

#### Monosaccharides

- **Single sugar units**; soluble, sweet, **reducing** if they have a free **carbonyl** able to participate in Benedict’s reaction (exam focus: **glucose** as the main example).
- **Hexoses** (6C): **α-glucose** and **β-glucose** are **isomers** differing in the position of the **-OH on C1** (ring form). **Starch and glycogen** use **α-glucose**; **cellulose** uses **β-glucose**.
- **Pentoses** (5C): **ribose** and **deoxyribose** (backbone of **RNA** and **DNA**—developed fully in the nucleic acids topic).

#### Condensation and hydrolysis

- **Condensation:** two monosaccharides form a **disaccharide** + **water**; the **covalent C–O–C link** is a **glycosidic bond** (formed between **-OH** groups, often with **enzyme** specificity in vivo).
- **Hydrolysis:** **water added**; bond breaks to regenerate monomers (**digestive enzymes** in gut, or **acid hydrolysis** in tests).

#### Disaccharides (examples)

| Disaccharide | Monomers | Glycosidic link (for awareness) | Notes |
|--------------|----------|----------------------------------|--------|
| **Maltose** | glucose + glucose | α-1,4 | **Reducing**; from starch digestion |
| **Sucrose** | glucose + fructose | α-1,2 | **Non-reducing** in Benedict’s test |
| **Lactose** | glucose + galactose | β-1,4 | **Reducing**; milk sugar |

#### Polysaccharides

- **Starch** (plants): storage; **amylose** (unbranched **α-1,4** helix) and **amylopectin** (branched, **α-1,6** branches). **Iodine** interacts with amylose for the **blue-black** colour.
- **Glycogen** (animals/fungi): highly **branched** **α-glucose** polymer; compact, rapid **glucose** release.
- **Cellulose** (plants): **unbranched** chains of **β-glucose** with **β-1,4** glycosidic bonds; parallel chains **cross-linked by hydrogen bonds** → **microfibrils** → **high tensile strength** in **cell walls**.

**Roles of carbohydrates**

- **Energy** (respiration substrate), **energy storage** (starch, glycogen), **structural** (cellulose, cell wall), and **precursors** for other molecules.

### Lipids

**Lipids** are a **diverse** group **insoluble in water** but soluble in **organic solvents** (e.g. ethanol)—used in the **emulsion test**.

#### Fatty acids

- Long **hydrocarbon** chains with a **terminal carboxyl** group.
- **Saturated:** **no C=C double bonds**; straight chains pack tightly → **higher melting point**, often **solid** at room temperature.
- **Unsaturated:** **one or more C=C** bonds; **cis** bends in chains → **less tight packing** → often **liquid** oils. **Hydrogenation** can convert unsaturated oils to more saturated fats (industrial / food context).

#### Triglycerides (triacylglycerols)

- **One glycerol** + **three fatty acids** joined by **ester bonds** (each forms by **condensation** with loss of water).
- **Roles:** **energy-dense** storage (oxidation releases much ATP), **insulation**, **protection** of organs; **waterproofing** (e.g. cuticle, mammal skin secretions when associated with other lipids).

#### Phospholipids

- **Glycerol**, **two fatty acid tails**, and a **phosphate-containing hydrophilic head**.
- **Amphipathic:** **hydrophobic tails** avoid water, **hydrophilic heads** interact with water.
- **Central to cell membranes** (developed in Topic 4): bilayer forms a **stable barrier** between **aqueous** compartments.

#### Steroids (awareness)

- **Cholesterol** is a **steroid** lipid important in **membrane fluidity** and as a **precursor** for steroid hormones (detail increases in membrane and hormone topics).

## 2.3 Proteins

### Amino acids and the peptide bond

- **~20 different amino acids** in proteins (for A Level you need the **general structure**: **central carbon (α-carbon)** bonded to **amino group (-NH₂)**, **carboxyl group (-COOH)**, **hydrogen**, and a **variable R group (side chain)**.
- **R group** determines **chemistry** of the residue: **polar**, **non-polar**, **acidic**, **basic**—this drives folding and function.

**Peptide bond:** **Condensation** between the **-COOH** of one amino acid and the **-NH₂** of another → **-CO-NH-** + water. A chain of many residues is a **polypeptide**; **protein** usually implies a **functional** folded molecule (sometimes more than one chain).

### Levels of structure

1. **Primary (1°):** **Sequence** of amino acids (determined genetically; **peptide bonds**).
2. **Secondary (2°):** **Regular repeating** local folding stabilised mainly by **hydrogen bonds** between backbone groups—**α-helix** and **β-pleated sheet** are standard examples.
3. **Tertiary (3°):** **Overall 3D shape** of one polypeptide, stabilised by **ionic**, **hydrogen**, **disulphide** (if cysteine present), and **hydrophobic interactions** (R groups).
4. **Quaternary (4°):** **More than one polypeptide chain** assembled (e.g. **haemoglobin** has **four** chains with **haem** groups for **oxygen** transport).

### Fibrous vs globular

- **Fibrous:** **elongated**, often **structural**, **insoluble** (e.g. **collagen** in connective tissues—**tensile strength**).
- **Globular:** **compact**, often **soluble**, **metabolic roles** (e.g. **enzymes**, **haemoglobin**).

**Denaturation:** loss of **3D structure** (and often function) by **extreme pH**, **temperature**, or **chemicals**—**bonds** maintaining tertiary/quaternary structure disrupted; **peptide bonds** usually remain intact unless **hydrolysed**.

## 2.4 Water

### Molecular polarity and hydrogen bonding

- **Water (H₂O)** is **polar**: **oxygen** attracts electrons more strongly → **partial δ−** on O, **partial δ+** on H.
- **Hydrogen bonds** form between **δ+ H** of one molecule and **δ− O** of another (weaker than covalent bonds, but **many** of them give water its special properties).

### Properties and biological roles

| Property | Cause (brief) | Biological significance |
|----------|----------------|-------------------------|
| **Excellent solvent** | Hydration shells around **ions** and **polar** molecules | **Metabolic reactions** in solution; **transport** of nutrients/wastes |
| **High specific heat capacity** | Energy absorbed to **break H-bonds** | **Stable temperatures** in cells and bodies; aquatic environments |
| **High latent heat of vapourisation** | Many H-bonds break on **evaporation** | **Cooling** by sweating |
| **Cohesion / adhesion** | H-bonds between water molecules; water **clings** to polar surfaces | **Water transport** in xylem (with **tension** and **adhesion** to walls) |
| **Density anomaly** | **Ice** is **less dense** than liquid water | **Ice floats** → aquatic life survives under frozen surfaces |
| **Transparent to visible light** | — | **Aquatic photosynthesis** possible below surface |

**Exam tip:** Link **structure** (polarity, H-bonding) to **property** to **role** in **one coherent chain** of reasoning.

## Integration and common misconceptions

- **Starch positive / Benedict negative** is consistent: starch is **not** a reducing sugar until **hydrolysed** to **glucose**.
- **Lipid present** does **not** mean positive Benedict’s; use the **emulsion** (or other lipid tests your practical scheme uses).
- **Protein** detection is **not** specific to one amino acid—it reflects **peptide bonds** (Biuret).
- **α- vs β-glucose** is **not** minor detail—it explains why **enzymes** that digest starch **do not** digest cellulose.
