## Why enzymes matter

Almost every metabolic pathway depends on **enzymes**—**globular proteins** (usually) that **speed up** reactions **without being used up**. Exam questions test **definitions**, **graphs**, **collision theory**, **denaturation**, **inhibition**, and **experimental design** (buffers, controls, repeats).

---

## 3.1 Mode of action of enzymes

### Basic properties

- **Biological catalysts** that increase the **rate** of a reaction by providing an **alternative pathway** with **lower activation energy** (the minimum energy colliding particles need to react).
- **Not consumed** in the overall reaction; **reused** many times.
- Often highly **specific** for one reaction or class of substrates (**substrate specificity**).

### Active site and substrate

- The **active site** is a **3D pocket** (formed by **tertiary** and sometimes **quaternary** structure) where the **substrate** binds.
- **Lock and key** (early model): rigid complementary shapes.
- **Induced fit** (refinement): the active site **changes shape slightly** as the substrate binds, improving fit and **straining** bonds in the substrate to favour reaction.

### Formation of products

1. **Substrate** binds to the **active site** → **enzyme–substrate (ES) complex**.
2. **Transition state** stabilised; **activation energy** lowered.
3. **Products** form and **leave**; enzyme returns to original shape (**free** to bind another substrate).

### Intracellular and extracellular enzymes

- **Intracellular:** e.g. enzymes of **glycolysis** in the **cytosol** or **matrix** enzymes in mitochondria.
- **Extracellular:** e.g. **digestive enzymes** (amylase, trypsin) secreted into the gut lumen.

### Cofactors and coenzymes (where required by your specification)

- Some enzymes need non-protein helpers: **cofactors** (ions such as **Zn²⁺**) or **coenzymes** (organic, e.g. **NAD**, **FAD**, **coenzyme A**) derived from vitamins in many cases.

## 3.2 Factors that affect enzyme action

Interpret all explanations with **collision theory** (more frequent **successful collisions** between enzyme and substrate) and **shape** of the **active site**.

### Temperature

- **Low temperature:** slow molecular motion → **fewer collisions** → **low rate**.
- **Rising temperature** (up to an **optimum**): more **kinetic energy** → more collisions and more molecules exceed activation energy → **rate increases**.
- **Above optimum:** **vibrational** energy breaks **weak bonds** maintaining **3D structure** → **denaturation** of the enzyme; **active site** shape lost → **rate falls sharply**.
- **Human** enzymes often have optimum near **~37 °C**; **thermophilic** organisms differ.

**Graph sketch:** rate vs temperature → **asymmetric peak** (rise then steep fall).

### pH

- **Ionisable** R-groups in and near the active site depend on **pH** for charge and shape.
- Each enzyme has an **optimum pH** (e.g. **pepsin** acidic; **trypsin** slightly alkaline).
- **Extreme pH** causes **denaturation** or **wrong charge pattern** → **lower rate**.

**Practical note:** use **buffers** when investigating pH so pH stays stable during the reaction.

### Substrate concentration

- At **low** [S], rate **increases** as [S] rises (more ES complexes form).
- When **all active sites** are **occupied** (**saturation**), rate reaches **Vmax** (maximum velocity); extra substrate **cannot** increase rate unless enzyme concentration increases.

**Graph:** **initial rate** vs [S] → **hyperbolic** rise levelling off (Michaelis–Menten style in advanced courses).

### Enzyme concentration

- Provided **substrate is in excess**, **initial rate** is **approximately proportional** to **enzyme concentration** (more active sites available).
- If substrate becomes **limiting**, rate is **no longer** proportional to enzyme concentration.

### Inhibitors

| Type | Binding site | Effect on Km / affinity | Effect on Vmax |
|------|----------------|-------------------------|----------------|
| **Competitive** | **Active site** (competes with substrate) | **Apparent Km increases** (needs more substrate to reach half Vmax) | **Unchanged** if [S] can be raised enough |
| **Non-competitive** (incl. many **uncompetitive** variants in broad teaching) | **Elsewhere**; alters **shape** | Often **unchanged** | **Lowered** (fewer functional enzyme molecules) |

- **End-product inhibition** (feedback): product binds **allosterically** and **slows** the pathway when product is abundant—**homeostatic** regulation.

### Immobilised enzymes (industrial / practical context)

- Enzymes trapped in **alginate beads** or bound to a matrix.
- **Advantages:** **reuse**, easier **separation** from product, often **greater stability**, continuous processing in **bioreactors**.

## Experimental skills (examiners’ favourites)

- **Initial rate:** measure **steepness early** in the curve (not end-point only) when comparing conditions.
- **Control variables:** **temperature**, **pH** (buffer), **volume**, **substrate batch**, **stirring**.
- **Repeats** and **mean**; identify **anomalies**; comment on **accuracy** (e.g. **water baths**, **thermometers**) and **precision**.
- **Safety:** **acid/alkali**, **hot water baths**, **biological hazards** (stated in question).

---

## Common misconceptions

- **“Enzyme used up”** — false in the **overall** stoichiometry; it is **regenerated**.
- **Optimum temperature** is **not** “when denaturation starts”—it is the **peak rate** before **significant** denaturation dominates.
- **Denaturation** is usually **permanent** for heat/pH extremes (unlike **competitive inhibition**, which is **reversible** if inhibitor is removed).
