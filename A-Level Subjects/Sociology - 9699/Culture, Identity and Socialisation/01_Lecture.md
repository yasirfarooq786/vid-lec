# Complete Lecture: Culture, Identity and Socialisation (Cambridge International AS & A Level Sociology 9699)

This lecture covers **culture**, **socialisation**, **identity**, and **social roles**—foundations for much of **9699**. Programme: [Cambridge 9699](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-sociology-9699/).

---

## 1. Culture and society

- **Culture:** shared beliefs, values, norms, symbols, and practices of a group.
- **Material culture** (objects, technology) and **non-material culture** (ideas, norms).
- **Subculture:** norms and values within a wider culture that distinguish a group.
- **High culture / popular culture** debates (access, power, taste).

---

## 2. Socialisation

**Socialisation** is the lifelong process through which individuals learn norms, values, roles, and identities.

- **Primary socialisation** (early years, family): basic language, emotional patterns, core identity.
- **Secondary socialisation** (school, peers, media, workplace): wider norms, specialist roles.

**Agencies of socialisation:** family, education, peer group, media, religion, workplace—each varies in **formality**, **control**, and **influence** by context.

---

## 3. Norms, values, and social control

- **Norms:** expected behaviour (often unwritten); **sanctions** (rewards/punishments) reinforce them.
- **Values:** deep beliefs about what is good/desirable.
- **Social control:** formal (law, police) and informal (shame, gossip, exclusion).

---

## 4. Identity

**Identity** can be analysed as:
- **Social roles** (student, sibling, worker).
- **Intersection** of gender, class, ethnicity, age, sexuality, disability.
- **Agency vs structure:** how far individuals **choose** identity vs how far it is **shaped** by society.

Theories often contrast **consensus** (shared norms integrate society) with **conflict** (identities shaped by power and inequality).

---

## 5. Theoretical lenses (overview)

- **Functionalism:** shared culture creates **social solidarity** and **value consensus**; socialisation transmits core values needed for stability.
- **Marxism:** dominant culture can reflect **ruling-class ideology**; socialisation may **reproduce** class relations.
- **Feminism:** gender socialisation and patriarchal norms shape identity and life chances.
- **Interactionism / social action:** identity is **negotiated** in everyday interaction (**labelling**, **presentation of self**).

Use named perspectives in essays and **compare** them on **evidence** and **evaluation**.

---

## 6. Examination skills

- **Define** culture, socialisation, identity precisely.
- **Apply** agencies to scenarios (e.g. how school might reinforce gender norms).
- **Evaluate** with methodology (whose experience is counted?) and with **cross-cultural** or **historical** examples.

---

## Common misconceptions

- Socialisation is **not** only childhood—it continues through life.
- **Culture** is not “the same as ethnicity”; it overlaps but is analytically distinct.
- **Identity** is not purely individual choice; structure and interaction both matter.
