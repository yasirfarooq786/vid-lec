# Complete Lecture: Family (Cambridge International AS & A Level Sociology 9699)

This lecture covers **family forms**, **roles**, **change**, and **theoretical debates** central to **9699**. Programme: [Cambridge 9699](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-sociology-9699/).

---

## 1. Key concepts

- **Family:** socially recognised relationships involving care, intimacy, and often kinship—definitions vary by time and place.
- **Household** vs **family:** not always the same unit (lodgers, multi-generational homes).
- **Kinship:** blood, marriage, adoption, chosen kin.
- **Diversity:** single-parent, reconstituted, same-sex couples, extended networks, global patterns.

---

## 2. Functions of the family (debates)

**Functionalist-style arguments (e.g. Parsons):**
- **Primary socialisation** and **stabilisation of adult personalities**.
- **Economic** cooperation (historically; still relevant in discussion).

**Marxist arguments:**
- Family **reproduces labour power**; may **consume** and **absorb** frustration linked to work.
- Inheritance and property can **reproduce class**.

**Feminist arguments:**
- **Triple burden**, domestic labour, unequal power; family as a site of **patriarchy** (varied by feminist strand).

**New Right / conservative arguments (where relevant):**
- Emphasis on **stable two-parent** norms; critique of “dependency” narratives (evaluate carefully).

---

## 3. Family change

Trends often discussed (use **data** and **nuance**):
- Later marriage, cohabitation, divorce/remarriage patterns.
- Smaller households, ageing populations, migration and transnational families.
- Changing gender roles (still **unequal** in many societies).

Explain **causes** (economic, legal, ideological, technology) and **effects** (on children, care, inequality)—avoid single-factor stories unless the question demands it.

---

## 4. Childhood and policy

- **Social construction of childhood** (cross-cultural and historical variation).
- State roles: education law, child protection, welfare—links to **social control** and **equality**.

---

## 5. How to write high-mark answers

1. **Define** the family concept used in the question.
2. **Theorise** with named perspectives and clear claims.
3. **Evidence**—statistics, trends, or recognised studies (as appropriate to the question).
4. **Evaluate**—alternative perspective, limitations of evidence, or global comparison.

---

## Common misconceptions

- “Decline of the family” oversimplifies **diversity** and **choice**.
- Functionalist and Marxist views are **not** interchangeable—keep assumptions distinct.
- **Patriarchy** operates at societal level; not every family member “chooses” inequality.
