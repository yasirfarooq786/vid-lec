# Voice-Over Script: Accounting Principles and Policies (Cambridge International AS & A Level Accounting 9706)

**[Visual: Building blocks labelled with concept names]**

**Narrator:**  
**Accounting principles** are the **ideas** behind the **rules**. **Going concern** assumes the business **keeps trading**. **Accrual accounting** matches **income** and **expenses** to the **right period**, not only **cash** dates.

---

**[Visual: Same policy year 1 and year 2 — “consistency”]**

**Narrator:**  
**Consistency** means using the **same methods** unless you have a **good reason** to **change**—so **trends** mean something. **Materiality** says **fix** what **matters** to **decisions**; **tiny** items can be **simplified**.

---

**[Visual: Prudence scale — not overstating assets]**

**Narrator:**  
**Prudence** traditionally warned against **over-optimistic** **assets** and **profits**. Modern standards also demand **neutrality**—accounts should **neither** hide **problems** **nor** create **secret** cushions.

---

**[Visual: Policy dropdown — straight line vs reducing balance]**

**Narrator:**  
An **accounting policy** is a **chosen method**, like **how** you **depreciate**. An **estimate** is a **judgement within** that policy, such as **useful life** or **residual value**. Exams often ask you to **defend** or **compare** policies.

---

**[Visual: IAS book spine — “awareness” tag]**

**Narrator:**  
At **A Level**, you need **awareness** that **international standards** shape **published** accounts—**inventory**, **non-current assets**, **revenue recognition**. Follow your **9706** materials for **how deep** to go.

---

**[Visual: Ethics — red line through “window dressing”]**

**Narrator:**  
**Ethical** reporting means **no** **window dressing** and **no** **bias** meant to **trick** users. **Integrity** and **objectivity** support a **true and fair** view.

---

**[Visual: Checklist — key concepts, policy vs estimate, ethics]**

**Narrator:**  
Learn **definitions** **word-perfect** for **short** questions, then **apply** them in **narrative** **interpretation** tasks. Next: **ratios** and **analysis**.
