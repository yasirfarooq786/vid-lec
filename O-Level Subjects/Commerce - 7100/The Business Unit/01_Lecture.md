## 1. What Exactly is a Business Unit?

At its heart, a **business unit** (or sometimes referred to as a **form of business ownership**) is simply the legal structure under which a business operates. Think of it as the 'clothing' a business wears – it dictates how it's owned, how it's managed, how profits are distributed, and crucially, the level of risk the owners face.

Understanding these different structures is vital because the choice of business unit profoundly impacts:
*   **How much capital (money) can be raised.**
*   **The level of control the owners have.**
*   **The amount of legal and administrative paperwork involved.**
*   **The personal risk (liability) the owners face.**
*   **The potential for growth and expansion.**

Excellent! Now that we have a clear idea of what we're looking at, let's explore the main types of business units you'll encounter in your studies and in the real world.

## 2. The Main Types of Business Units (Forms of Ownership)

We're going to explore the four most common and important forms of business ownership that you absolutely need to master for your Cambridge Commerce 7100 exam. Get ready!

### 2.1. The Sole Trader (or Sole Proprietorship)

Let's start with the simplest and most common form of business unit.

**Definition:** A **sole trader** is a business owned and controlled by **one person**. This individual provides the capital, takes all the risks, and receives all the profits.

**Imagine this:** *Your local baker who bakes delicious bread and runs their own small shop, or a freelance graphic designer working from home.* These are classic sole traders!

#### Key Characteristics:
*   **One Owner:** As the name suggests, it's just one individual.
*   **Easy to Set Up:** Minimal legal formalities. Often, you just need to register your business name and start trading.
*   **Owner Keeps All Profits:** Every penny of profit, after expenses, belongs to the sole trader.
*   **Unlimited Liability:** This is a **CRUCIAL** point. It means there's no legal distinction between the owner and the business. If the business incurs debts, the owner's personal assets (like their house, car, savings) can be used to pay those debts. It's a significant risk!
*   **Full Control:** The owner makes all the decisions.
*   **Limited Access to Capital:** Raising large sums of money can be challenging, often relying on personal savings or small bank loans.

#### Advantages:
*   **Easy to Start and End:** Low set-up costs and fewer legal hurdles.
*   **Owner Keeps All Profits:** A powerful motivator!
*   **Full Control:** The owner is the boss, making all decisions quickly.
*   **Privacy:** No need to publish financial accounts.
*   **Personalised Service:** Often allows for close relationships with customers.

#### Disadvantages:
*   **Unlimited Liability:** The biggest drawback! High personal risk.
*   **Limited Access to Capital:** Can hinder growth and expansion.
*   **Heavy Workload:** The owner has to manage everything (marketing, finance, operations, sales).
*   **Lack of Continuity:** The business might cease to exist if the owner falls ill, retires, or passes away.
*   **Limited Skills:** One person might not have expertise in all areas of business.

**Think about it:** Would you want to be a sole trader? What aspects appeal to you, and what makes you cautious?

### 2.2. The Partnership

Now, what if our sole trader friend wants to share the workload, raise more capital, or bring in complementary skills? They might consider a partnership!

**Definition:** A **partnership** is a business owned and controlled by **two or more people** (usually between 2 and 20, though specific limits can vary by country) who agree to share profits and losses.

**Imagine this:** *A small law firm with two lawyers, or a group of friends who open a café together, pooling their money and skills.*

#### Key Characteristics:
*   **Multiple Owners:** Typically 2-20 partners.
*   **Partnership Agreement (Deed of Partnership):** While not legally required to start, it's highly recommended! This document outlines how profits/losses will be shared, responsibilities, capital contributions, and what happens if a partner leaves.
*   **Shared Profits/Losses:** Agreed upon in the partnership agreement.
*   **Unlimited Liability:** **Again, this is crucial!** Most general partnerships have unlimited liability, meaning each partner is personally responsible for the business's debts, even if another partner incurred them. This is a significant risk. (Note: Some countries have 'Limited Liability Partnerships' (LLPs) where liability is limited, but for Cambridge 7100, generally assume unlimited liability for standard partnerships unless specified).
*   **More Capital:** Easier to raise capital than a sole trader, as multiple individuals contribute.
*   **Combined Skills:** Partners bring diverse expertise to the business.

#### Advantages:
*   **Easier to Set Up than a Company:** Fewer legal formalities than a limited company.
*   **More Capital Available:** Compared to a sole trader.
*   **Shared Workload and Skills:** Partners can specialise and support each other.
*   **Shared Decision-Making:** Can lead to better decisions through discussion.
*   **Privacy:** No need to publish financial accounts (like sole traders).

#### Disadvantages:
*   **Unlimited Liability:** Just like a sole trader, partners risk their personal assets.
*   **Potential for Disagreements:** Partners might have different visions or work ethics.
*   **Profits are Shared:** Less profit for each individual compared to a successful sole trader.
*   **Lack of Continuity:** If a partner leaves or dies, the partnership might need to be dissolved or re-formed.
*   **Slow Decision-Making:** More people involved can mean longer discussions.

**Excellent progress!** We've covered the individual and the group of individuals. Now, let's look at business units that offer a different level of protection to their owners.

### 2.3. The Private Limited Company (Pvt Ltd / Ltd)

This is where things get a bit more formal and exciting!

**Definition:** A **private limited company** is a separate legal entity from its owners (shareholders). Its shares are not offered to the general public, and its liability is limited.

**Imagine this:** *A family-owned manufacturing business, a medium-sized software development company, or a chain of local restaurants that has grown beyond a partnership.*

#### Key Characteristics:
*   **Separate Legal Personality:** This is a **HUGE** difference! The company is seen as its own 'person' in the eyes of the law. It can own assets, incur debts, and enter into contracts in its own name, separate from its owners.
*   **Limited Liability:** **The most significant advantage!** Shareholders are only liable for the amount they have invested in the company (the value of their shares). Their personal assets are protected if the company goes bankrupt.
*   **Owned by Shareholders:** Individuals or other companies who have bought shares in the company.
*   **Managed by a Board of Directors:** Shareholders elect directors to manage the company on their behalf. Directors can also be shareholders.
*   **Shares Not Publicly Traded:** Shares can only be sold privately, often requiring agreement from other shareholders.
*   **More Formalities:** Requires registration with a government body (e.g., Registrar of Companies) and adherence to company law (e.g., filing annual accounts).
*   **Memorandum of Association & Articles of Association:** Key legal documents outlining the company's purpose and internal rules.

#### Advantages:
*   **Limited Liability:** Protects shareholders' personal assets.
*   **Easier to Raise Capital:** Can sell more shares to a wider (private) audience.
*   **Greater Continuity:** The company continues to exist even if shareholders or directors change.
*   **Professional Image:** Often seen as more credible and stable.
*   **Potential for Growth:** Structure allows for significant expansion.

#### Disadvantages:
*   **More Complex and Costly to Set Up:** Significant legal and administrative requirements.
*   **Less Privacy:** Financial accounts must be filed and are publicly accessible.
*   **Slower Decision-Making:** Involves board meetings and formal procedures.
*   **Profits are Shared:** Among all shareholders, often through dividends.
*   **Greater Regulation:** Must comply with company laws and regulations.

You're doing brilliantly! We're almost through the main types. One more to go, and it's the biggest one!

### 2.4. The Public Limited Company (PLC / Plc)

This is the pinnacle for many businesses, allowing them to raise massive amounts of capital!

**Definition:** A **public limited company** is a company whose shares are offered and traded on a stock exchange to the general public. Like a private limited company, it has separate legal personality and limited liability.

**Imagine this:** *Large multinational corporations like Apple, Coca-Cola, or your national airline.* These are typically public limited companies.

#### Key Characteristics:
*   **Shares Traded on a Stock Exchange:** The defining feature! Anyone can buy and sell shares through the stock market.
*   **Large Capital:** Can raise enormous amounts of capital from millions of investors worldwide.
*   **Separate Legal Personality & Limited Liability:** Just like a private limited company, but on a grander scale.
*   **Managed by a Board of Directors:** Appointed by shareholders.
*   **Strict Regulations:** Subject to rigorous rules and scrutiny from stock exchanges and government bodies to protect investors.
*   **Minimum Share Capital:** Often a legal requirement to have a minimum amount of share capital before going public.

#### Advantages:
*   **Vast Capital Raising Potential:** Can raise huge sums of money for expansion from the public.
*   **Enhanced Public Image:** Often seen as highly reputable and stable.
*   **Limited Liability:** Protects shareholders.
*   **Easy Transferability of Shares:** Shares can be bought and sold freely on the stock exchange.
*   **Greater Continuity:** Existence is independent of owners/managers.

#### Disadvantages:
*   **Extremely Complex and Costly to Set Up:** Involves extensive legal processes (e.g., initial public offering - IPO).
*   **Loss of Control for Original Owners:** Ownership can become diluted among many shareholders.
*   **High Level of Scrutiny:** Constant public and media attention, pressure from shareholders for short-term profits.
*   **Less Privacy:** Detailed financial information is publicly available and scrutinised.
*   **Vulnerable to Takeovers:** Other companies can buy enough shares to gain control.

## 3. Factors Affecting the Choice of Business Unit

Now that we understand the different types, how does an entrepreneur or a group of individuals decide which one is right for them? It's a strategic decision based on several key factors:

1.  **Amount of Capital Required:**
    *   **Sole Trader/Partnership:** Suitable for businesses requiring less capital (personal savings, small loans).
    *   **Private Ltd/Public Ltd:** Ideal for businesses needing substantial capital for growth and expansion (selling shares).

2.  **Level of Risk / Liability:**
    *   **Sole Trader/Partnership:** High personal risk due to **unlimited liability**.
    *   **Private Ltd/Public Ltd:** Lower personal risk due to **limited liability** (personal assets are protected).

3.  **Degree of Control Desired:**
    *   **Sole Trader:** Full control.
    *   **Partnership:** Shared control, but still direct.
    *   **Private Ltd:** Control often rests with major shareholders and the board.
    *   **Public Ltd:** Control can be diffused among many shareholders; original owners may lose significant control.

4.  **Ease and Cost of Formation:**
    *   **Sole Trader:** Easiest and cheapest to set up.
    *   **Partnership:** Relatively easy, but a partnership agreement is recommended.
    *   **Private Ltd:** More complex and costly due to legal requirements.
    *   **Public Ltd:** Most complex, time-consuming, and expensive to establish.

5.  **Number of Owners:**
    *   **Sole Trader:** One owner.
    *   **Partnership:** 2-20 owners.
    *   **Companies (Ltd/PLC):** Can have many shareholders (even millions for a PLC).

6.  **Size and Growth Potential:**
    *   **Sole Trader/Partnership:** Often suitable for small-to-medium-sized businesses with limited growth aspirations.
    *   **Private Ltd/Public Ltd:** Designed for larger businesses with significant growth potential and a need for substantial capital.

7.  **Tax Implications:** (Briefly) Different business units have different tax structures. Companies often pay corporation tax, while sole traders and partners pay income tax on their profits. This is a complex area but worth noting as a factor.

## 4. Key Concepts to Master

Before we wrap up, let's consolidate some absolute must-know concepts:

*   **Unlimited Liability:** The owner(s) are personally responsible for all business debts. Their personal assets are at risk. (Sole Trader, Partnership)
*   **Limited Liability:** The owner(s) are only liable up to the amount they have invested in the business (e.g., the value of their shares). Their personal assets are protected. (Private Limited Company, Public Limited Company)
*   **Separate Legal Personality:** The business is legally distinct from its owners. It can sue, be sued, own assets, and incur debts in its own name. (Private Limited Company, Public Limited Company)
*   **Shareholders:** The owners of a company, who hold shares.
*   **Directors:** Individuals appointed by shareholders to manage the company on a day-to-day basis.
*   **Capital:** The money or assets invested in a business.
